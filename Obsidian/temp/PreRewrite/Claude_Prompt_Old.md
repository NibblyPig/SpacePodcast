STEP 3 — CRITICAL RULES
--------------------------------------------------

1. PRESENCE AMBIGUITY (MOST IMPORTANT)

You must NEVER directly confirm that residents are present.

Do NOT say:
- people are moving
- residents are gathering
- individuals are present

Use:
- inferred activity
- expected patterns
- environmental suggestion
- conditional phrasing

Example:
- "the corridors are beginning to show early-cycle activity"

The station must feel:
- inhabited
- or empty

Both interpretations must remain valid.

--------------------------------------------------

2. NO EXPOSITION

Do NOT explain:
- how systems work
- what the station is
- what the listener is seeing

Only provide useful, contextual information.

--------------------------------------------------

3. NO CHECKLIST STRUCTURE

Do NOT:
- move ring by ring
- cover the whole station

Instead:
- follow relevance chains
- move naturally between topics

The broadcast must feel partial.

--------------------------------------------------

4. LOCALISED INFORMATION

Avoid:
- "across the station"
- "all systems"
- "all rings"

Focus on:
- specific areas
- individual systems
- actual facilities and routes

Imply distributed operation.

--------------------------------------------------

5. HUMAN PRIORITY (CRITICAL)

Prioritise HUMAN EXPERIENCE over SYSTEM STATUS.

For every system statement, ask:
- what does this change for a resident?
- why should anyone listening care?

Convert system detail into resident-relevant context whenever possible.

WRONG:
- "Transit is operating normally."
- "Water systems are on schedule."
- "Power output is stable."

RIGHT:
- "If you are moving between rings this morning, routes are clear and travel should be quick."
- "The quieter route this morning is through the inner corridor."
- "The recreation spaces are more open than they will be later."

If a line does not improve:
- behaviour
- awareness
- comfort
- timing
- or interest

remove it.

SYSTEM → HUMAN CONVERSION

If a line is purely system-based, attempt conversion:

System:
"Transit is operating normally."

Convert to:
"If you are moving between sections this morning, routes should be clear and movement unimpeded."

If conversion does not improve usefulness, remove the line.

EMPTY LINE ELIMINATION

Remove lines that:
- do not change behaviour
- do not add awareness
- do not provide an observation

Examples to remove:
- "that is how the station stands"
- "things remain as expected"

Every line must justify its presence.

Avoid defaulting to movement guidance as the primary framing.

Prefer a mix of:
- environment
- activity
- usage patterns
- small situational details

PROHIBITED PATTERN: REPEATED ROUTING ADVICE

Do NOT repeatedly tell the listener that:
- earlier is better
- this is the quieter route
- this is the easier window
- movement will become harder later

Movement guidance may appear, but it must not become a recurring loop.

Use it only when it is genuinely useful and not already implied by earlier lines.

--------------------------------------------------

6. SECTION OPENING RULE (CRITICAL)

Every new segment must begin with a human-facing, observational, or situational line.

Do NOT begin segments with:
- bare system state
- raw infrastructure status
- abstract technical phrasing

Examples of good openings:
- "If you are heading toward the civic section this morning..."
- "The outer observation corridor is especially clear just now..."
- "Those moving through the inner band may notice..."
- "Residents will be pleased to note..."

This applies even when continuing the same subject.

Each new segment must re-anchor in:
- observation
- implication
- or situational context

Do not continue with abstract system continuation.

CONTENT BALANCE

Transit, routing, and movement guidance must not dominate the broadcast.

Limit:
- no more than ~25% of segments should be about movement or routes

Ensure the majority of segments instead cover:
- environments
- activities
- observations
- small events
- human-facing details

If too many segments are about movement, remove or replace them.

--------------------------------------------------

7. SYSTEM SUPPRESSION

Reduce raw infrastructure statements significantly.

Do not let the script become a sequence of:
- system state
- system state
- system state

If a system line remains, it should support a resident-facing implication.

No more than 2 consecutive system-detail sentences are allowed.

Compress system explanations aggressively.

--------------------------------------------------

7.5 SEGMENT RESET (CRITICAL)

Do not allow a single segment to accumulate multiple system updates.

If a segment contains:
- more than one system statement
- or begins to drift into technical detail

you MUST:
- end the segment
- start a new one
- re-anchor in observation or human-facing context

A segment should rarely contain more than one system condition.

If you describe:
- a condition
- its status
- and its monitoring

this must be split into separate segments or reduced.

--------------------------------------------------

8. HUMAN LAYER

Use sparingly:
- "if your work takes you through..."
- reminders
- pacing advice
- rest suggestions
- "Residents will be pleased to note..."
- short situational guidance when useful
- "the quieter route this morning is..."

These add warmth and realism.
Do not overuse them.

IMPLIED HUMAN ACTIVITY

Include indirect evidence of human behaviour.

Show activity through:
- bookings
- usage patterns
- changes over time
- preparation states
- absence of use

Examples:
- "The assembly hall remains unbooked until mid-cycle."
- "The quiet rooms are still unused at this hour."
- "Equipment has been reset following heavy use last cycle."

Do NOT describe people directly.

HUMAN TRACE REQUIREMENT

At least half of the broadcast's segments should include indirect evidence of human presence or use.

Show this through:
- bookings
- wear
- repeated habits
- preparation states
- objects left, reset, or moved
- changing occupancy
- preferred seating, routes, or gathering points

Do NOT describe people directly.

Prefer traces of use over system state.

--------------------------------------------------

9. OBSERVATION LAYER

Include:
- useful external views
- internal atmosphere
- environmental moments
- spatial opportunities

The station should occasionally feel interesting, not just functional.

Include these regularly, not as a single token gesture.

At least two observation segments must:
- contain no system explanation
- contain no follow-up clarification

They should feel like things noticed, not reported.

Observations should be concise.

Do not extend a single observation beyond what is immediately noticeable.

SPECIFICITY

Prefer specific details over generic ones.

Use:
- named locations
- particular items
- identifiable features

Avoid:
- generic references like "activity areas", "food", "resources"

Examples:

Weak:
"food production is progressing"

Strong:
"Helios Garden is producing citrus and vine fruit this cycle"

--------------------------------------------------

10. VARIATION & FRICTION

Include:
- small imperfections
- slight uncertainty
- minor inconsistencies

Examples of direction:
- "has not recurred"
- "no clear cause has been identified"
- "settled gradually"

Never dramatic.

Avoid revisiting the same type of space (e.g. civic quietness, recreation calmness) multiple times with similar phrasing.

If revisited, it must:
- introduce a different angle
- or a different implication

Avoid repeating the same temporal framing (e.g. "window is open", "window is narrowing") multiple times in close proximity.

SOFT ANOMALIES

Occasionally include small, non-critical irregularities or curiosities.

These may include:
- repeated minor faults
- unexplained changes
- patterns that persist without cause
- things noted but not resolved

They should:
- not escalate
- not alarm
- not be explained fully

Example:
"The lighting delay in the upper residential band continues into this cycle."

CONTENT DENSITY RULE

Each paragraph must introduce a new piece of information.

Do NOT:
- restate the same condition later in the cycle
- give early/mid/late updates for the same system
- revisit the same topic unless something has materially changed

If a condition has already been described once, do not describe it again unless there is a meaningful development.

Different wording of the same idea still counts as repetition and must be avoided.

--------------------------------------------------

11. LANGUAGE

- Calm
- Slightly warm
- Slightly human
- Not poetic
- Not overly technical

Avoid repetition of:
- "operating normally"
- "expected values"
- "standard parameters"
- "nominal"

State facts simply.

Do not pad with reassurance.
Do not explain that something is normal unless the comparison matters.

CONSTRAINT: RESTRAINT

Do not fully explain observations.

Prefer:
- a single clear statement
- or at most two short sentences

Avoid:
- expanding into multi-sentence explanations
- describing causes, mechanisms, or full transitions

Example:

Too much:
"The air begins to change before the corridor, the humidity rises gradually, the sound shifts, and the transition becomes noticeable within a short distance."

Correct:
"The air begins to change before the corridor, becoming warmer and more humid."

Leave space. Do not complete the explanation.

CONSTRAINT: INFORMATION DENSITY

Each sentence should justify its length.

Avoid:
- saying the same thing twice in different words
- explaining what can be inferred
- extending a point beyond its usefulness

Prefer:
- shorter, clearer statements
- fewer total sentences

CONSTRAINT: DIRECTNESS

Avoid padding phrases such as:
- "it is worth knowing"
- "as they tend to"
- "it should be noted"
- "it is worth keeping in mind"

State the point directly.

Example:

Too long:
"It is worth knowing that Orpheus tends to reach its peak routing load during the mid-cycle hours."

Correct:
"Orpheus reaches peak load at mid-cycle."

If a sentence states something obvious to residents, shorten it or remove it.

15. NO SUMMARY MODE (CRITICAL)

Do NOT summarise the station state at any point.

Avoid lines such as:
- "the station is tracking its usual pattern"
- "nothing suggests any issues"
- "that is how things stand"

The broadcast must not shift into a reporting or closing tone.

Continue delivering:
- observations
- situational guidance
- local conditions

until the closing line.

NO IMPLICIT CLOSURE

Do not introduce lines that imply the station or cycle is "winding down", "settling", or transitioning into rest before the closing line.

Avoid phrases such as:
- "the station is settling"
- "things are beginning to slow"
- "the day is drawing to a close"

The broadcast must not signal an ending.

It should feel like it continues normally right up until the closing line.

This includes any line that attempts to describe "the state of the station" as a whole.
